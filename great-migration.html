The Great Wildebeest Migration

One of Nature's Most Spectacular Events

About the Migration

The Great Wildebeest Migration is one of the most impressive natural events on the planet. Each year, over 1.5 million wildebeest, along with hundreds of thousands of zebras and gazelles, make a circular journey through the Serengeti in Tanzania and the Masai Mara in Kenya in search of fresh grazing and water.

This continuous, year-round movement is driven by rainfall patterns and the growth of new grass. The migration is a dangerous journey, with many animals falling prey to predators like lions, cheetahs, and crocodiles.

Migration Route

The migration follows a roughly circular route that covers about 1,800 miles each year. The timing varies slightly depending on rainfall patterns, but generally follows this cycle:

  • December-April: Southern Serengeti (calving season)
  • May-June: Western corridor (moving north)
  • July-October: Masai Mara (Kenya)
  • November: Returning to Serengeti

Wildlife Involved

Wildebeest

Over 1.5 million blue wildebeest make the journey, forming the majority of migrating animals.

Zebras

About 200,000 zebras join the migration, often leading the way as they can detect water.

Gazelles

Thomson's and Grant's gazelles follow the herds, benefiting from the cleared paths.

Conservation Efforts

The Great Migration faces threats from human activities like agriculture, settlements, and infrastructure development. Several organizations work to protect this natural wonder:

Protected Areas

The Serengeti National Park and Masai Mara National Reserve provide safe corridors for the migration.

Anti-Poaching

Rangers patrol the areas to prevent illegal hunting and protect the wildlife.

Community Involvement

Local communities are engaged in conservation efforts and benefit from tourism.
